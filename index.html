What the test requires
  • 200-character plain-language passage
  • Letters, digits, punctuation (. , ; – ?)
  • 5 minutes continuous at 8 wpm
  • Max 5 errors allowed
  • 1 min error-free = automatic pass
  • Receive AND send in same session
  • 1-min practice run at start (ungraded)
Training milestones
  • Week 1–2: Learn all characters
  • Week 3: Copy callsigns at 4 wpm
  • Week 4: Plain text at 5–6 wpm
  • Week 5: Plain text at 7 wpm
  • Week 6–7: Consistent 8+ wpm
  • Week 8: Full 5-min sessions daily
Train receiving at 10–12 wpm so 8 wpm feels comfortable. Use the Koch method — start with 2 characters, add one only when you hit 90% accuracy. Practice sending on a straight key daily — muscle memory matters as much as ear training.

SectionSummary
Sec 3No person shall possess wireless telegraphy apparatus without a licence
Sec 4Central Govt may exempt persons or classes of persons from the Act

Understanding Q codes as questions and answers

Every Q code does double duty. Without a question mark it is a statement. Add a ? and it becomes the corresponding question — the same three letters, flipped by a single symbol.

The flip mechanic — worked examples
CodeAs a statementAs a question (?)
QRTI am suspending operation / closing downShall I cease transmitting?
QRMI am being interfered with (man-made)Are you being interfered with?
QSLI acknowledge receipt (confirmed)Can you acknowledge receipt?
QTHMy location is … (place or lat/long)What is your location?
QRVI am ready to receive / operateAre you ready?
QSYChange your transmission frequency to … kHzShall I change my transmission frequency?
Confusable pairs — the exam loves these
PairHow to tell them apart
QRM vs QRNQRM = Man-made interference. QRN = Natural noise (static, lightning). M for Man, N for Nature.
QRO vs QRPQRO = increase power. QRP = decrease power. Hams also use QRP informally to mean low-power operating.
QSL vs QSOQSL = acknowledgement / confirmation (like a receipt). QSO = a two-way contact. You have a QSO; you send a QSL card to confirm it.
QRK vs QSAQRK = readability (how clearly you copy, 1–5). QSA = signal strength (how strong, 1–5). Same scale, different qualities.
QRX vs QRVQRX = stand by / wait. QRV = I am ready now. QRX asks you to wait; QRV says go ahead.
QRI vs QRHQRI = tone quality (good / variable / bad). QRH = frequency stability (is it drifting?). QRI is about sound; QRH is about frequency.
Grouping by middle letter
QR_ — operating conditions

Frequency, interference, power, readability, tone, stopping, readiness — everything about the state of a live transmission.

QS_ — scheduling & contacts

Sending, acknowledgement, contacts (QSO), frequency changes (QSY), listening (QSX), relaying (QSU) — arranging and confirming contacts.

QT_ — time & location

QTH = location. QTR = correct time. QTC = messages. Small group, easy to learn as a set.

A typical QSO exchange — Q codes in context
VU2XYZ: CQ CQ CQ DE VU2XYZ VU2XYZ K ← general call; DE = from; K = go ahead (any station)
VU2ABC: VU2XYZ DE VU2ABC QRK? QSA? ← how is my readability / signal strength?
VU2XYZ: VU2ABC DE VU2XYZ QRK 4 QSA 4 QTH? ← readability 4, strength 4. What is your location?
VU2ABC: QTH Mumbai QRM? QRN? ← my location is Mumbai. Do you have interference / static?
VU2XYZ: QRM 2 QRN NIL QSY 14225 C? ← some man-made interference, no static. Move to 14.225 MHz?
VU2ABC: C QSL QRT AR ← yes, confirmed, closing down, end of transmission
In the exam, match the direction: telling someone your location = QTH (statement); asking for theirs = QTH? (question).
CW abbreviations — in context

Unlike Q codes, CW abbreviations are not question/answer pairs — each has one fixed meaning. They are procedural signals that structure a Morse exchange.

Procedural flow
Step 1 — Call: CQ CQ DE VU2XYZ K ← CQ = calling all; DE = from; K = go ahead (any station)
Step 2 — Reply: VU2XYZ DE VU2ABC R K ← R = received; K = go ahead (you specifically)
Step 3 — Exchange: CFM QTH Delhi TU ← CFM = confirm; TU = thank you
Step 4 — Close: OK TU 73 VA ← OK = agreed; VA = end of work / final sign-off
If interrupted: AS AS ← AS = wait / stand by
If closing station: QRT CL AR ← QRT = suspending; CL = closing station; AR = end of tx
AR vs VA vs K — the three endings
CodeMeaningWhen to use
AREnd of transmissionEnd of a single message. The contact may continue.
VAEnd of work / final sign-offThe entire QSO is over. Often followed by CL if going off air.
KGo ahead / overInvitation to transmit — not an ending, an invitation.
AA / AB / WA / WB — the message-editing group
Sender: THE MEETING IS AT 1400 HOURS
Receiver: AA HOURS ← All after HOURS — repeat everything after the word HOURS
Receiver: AB 1400 ← All before 1400 — repeat everything before 1400
Receiver: WA MEETING← Word after MEETING — what is the next word?
Receiver: WB 1400 ← Word before 1400 — what is the preceding word?
NIL = nothing for you / no traffic. C = yes / correct / affirmative. Both are complete messages on their own — don't confuse them.
Distress & urgency signals (ITU Articles 30–33)

The ITU defines a strict three-level priority hierarchy. All other traffic must cease when a distress or urgency call is in progress.

CategoryVoiceMorseMeaning
DistressMAYDAYSOS (···−−−···)Grave and imminent danger — immediate assistance required. Sent three times.
UrgencyPAN PANXXXUrgent — safety of vessel or person, not immediately life-threatening.
SafetySÉCURITÉTTTImportant navigational or meteorological warning. Lowest of the three.
Key rules for the exam
Distress frequency: 500 kHz (CW) and 2182 kHz (voice) internationally
SOS is sent as a continuous sequence (···−−−···) — not three separate letters with gaps between them
The distress call must be sent three times, followed by DE, then the station call sign (also three times)
All stations hearing a distress call must immediately cease transmitting on that frequency and listen
Only the station in distress, or a station authorised by it, may cancel the distress traffic
QUM means: may I resume normal working? — sent to the controlling station after distress traffic ends
